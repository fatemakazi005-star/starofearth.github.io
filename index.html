01 — Definition

What Is a
Black Hole?

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so extreme that nothing — not even light — can escape once it crosses the boundary known as the event horizon.

They form when massive stars collapse at the end of their lifecycle, compressing enormous mass into an infinitely dense point called a singularity.

Black holes warp the fabric of spacetime around them, creating effects like gravitational time dilation, gravitational lensing, and spaghettification.

02 — Classification

Types of Black Holes

Not all black holes are equal. They span an astonishing range of sizes, from a few solar masses to billions.

⚫
Stellar
3 – 20 × M☉

Born from the gravitational collapse of a dying massive star. The most commonly detected type via gravitational-wave observatories and X-ray telescopes.

🌀
Supermassive
10⁶ – 10¹⁰ × M☉

Reside at the centers of almost all large galaxies, including our own Milky Way. M87* and Sgr A* have been directly imaged. Their formation is still debated.

💠
Intermediate
10² – 10⁵ × M☉

The most elusive class. Candidates have been found in dense star clusters. They may be the missing link between stellar and supermassive black holes.

03 — Knowledge Base

Mind-Bending Facts

01
Time Dilation

Near a black hole, time passes slower relative to a distant observer. An astronaut hovering near the event horizon for hours might return to find decades have passed on Earth — a phenomenon predicted by Einstein's General Relativity.

02
Hawking Radiation

Stephen Hawking theorized that black holes slowly emit thermal radiation due to quantum mechanical effects near the event horizon — meaning black holes can eventually evaporate over unimaginably long timescales.

03
Spaghettification

Tidal forces near a black hole are so intense they would stretch any object — including a human — into a long, thin strand of matter. The effect is stronger and faster near smaller black holes due to steeper gravity gradients.

04
No Hair Theorem

Black holes are described entirely by just three measurable properties: mass, electric charge, and spin. All other information about the matter that formed them is inaccessible — a puzzle known as the Information Paradox.

05
Gravitational Lensing

A black hole's gravity bends light paths around it, acting as a cosmic magnifying lens. The effect can create multiple distorted images of background galaxies, arcs, and even complete "Einstein Rings."

06
First Photograph

In April 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope — a planet-wide network of radio observatories — captured the first direct image of a black hole: the supermassive M87*, located 55 million light-years from Earth.

04 — Discovery

Historical Timeline

1783
Dark Stars Proposed

John Michell proposed "dark stars" — objects so massive that even light cannot escape. A remarkably prescient idea, centuries before the physics to describe it existed.

1915
General Relativity

Einstein's field equations described how mass warps spacetime — and Karl Schwarzschild derived the first exact solution, describing what we now call a black hole.

1967
"Black Hole" Coined

Physicist John Wheeler popularized the term "black hole" at a conference in New York, replacing the unwieldy "gravitationally completely collapsed star."

1974
Hawking Radiation

Stephen Hawking showed that black holes are not entirely black — quantum effects cause them to radiate, slowly losing mass over cosmic timescales.

2015
Gravitational Waves Detected

LIGO detected ripples in spacetime from two black holes merging 1.3 billion light-years away — confirming a 100-year-old prediction and opening a new era of astronomy.

2019
First Black Hole Image

The Event Horizon Telescope produced history's first direct image of a black hole — M87* — using a network of observatories spanning the entire planet.

2022
Milky Way's Black Hole Imaged

The EHT released the first image of Sagittarius A*, the supermassive black hole at the center of our own galaxy, 27,000 light-years from Earth.
